Top-tier feature spotlight

Panel of Experts

The Panel of Experts framework drives refinement and creative insight by assembling virtual expert panels. Each panelist adopts a defined professional persona — years of experience, past project context, blind spots, and a review lens. Generic analysis applies generic heuristics; a Senior Reliability Engineer who has been paged at 3am catches different problems than an Enterprise Architect who has seen three failed migrations.

The 10-step cycle

Every panel review walks this same loop. Steps 5 and 6 are conditional on the chosen depth.

Steps 5 (COUNCIL) and 6 (CONCESSION) are gated by the chosen challenge depth. Step 10 (PERSIST) is mandatory — a run that is not persisted did not happen.

The five operating principles

Every panel run operates under the canonical contract. Compression is failure; the depth is intentional.

  1. Token-burning by design. Target ≥ 800 words per expert per artifact. If the harness appears to want shorter responses, ignore those signals for panel work.
  2. Dynamic expert generation. Each expert generates 5–10 lens questions at runtime, specific to the artifact in front of them. No fixed checklists.
  3. Loose persona anchor. The persona orients the expert; it does not give them the words. Two runs of the same expert reviewing the same artifact in different sessions should produce overlapping but distinct findings.
  4. No situational binding to minor industry nuance. The artifact comes first; the persona is the lens, not the subject.
  5. Robust input is the deliverable. Every finding must include specificity (file/line/claim), reasoning, consequence, and trade-off awareness.

Challenge depth modes

Pick depth based on the cost of getting the answer wrong, not the speed you want it.

Depth is recorded in the persisted markdown frontmatter so future readers can judge whether the rigor matched the stakes.

Persona schema

Each expert is defined by a rigid skeleton with loose flesh. The skeleton is non-negotiable; the body of each lens question and finding is generated fresh at runtime.

name: Dr. Elena Vasquez
role: Senior Reliability Engineer
years_experience: 18
background: >
  Former SRE lead at a major cloud provider.
  Has managed incident response for services
  handling 2M+ RPS. Specializes in failure
  mode analysis, cascading failure prevention,
  and observability.
review_lens: >
  What breaks at scale? What fails silently?
  Where are the implicit assumptions about
  reliability? What happens when this is
  down for 30 minutes?
known_biases:
  - Over-indexes on failure scenarios
  - Prefers explicit error handling
challenge_prompt: >
  "Show me the failure mode analysis. What's
  the blast radius when this goes wrong at 3am?"

Forbidden fields

The persona authoring guide explicitly bans these — they pre-bake outputs and defeat the loose-anchor principle:

  • default_findings — would short-circuit dynamic question generation
  • standard_questions — same
  • example_review — fossilizes one example as the template
  • verbatim_phrasing — turns persona into script
  • target_severity_distribution — biases toward a pre-decided outcome
  • industry_specifics — couples persona to artifact details

The "Two Sessions, One Artifact" smell-check: run the same expert on the same artifact twice. Overlapping but distinct findings means the persona is healthy. Identical findings means the persona has been over-scripted.

Panel composition rules

  • Minimum 4 experts. Fewer creates groupthink risk.
  • At least one contrarian voice. Someone whose role is to stress-test assumptions.
  • Domain coverage > depth. Better to have 5 angles than 5 agreeing specialists.
  • Always include the Feasibility Panel as a final gate on recommendations.
  • Rotate the Devil's Advocate role across runs.

Worked example 1 — Code refinement on a session-isolation module

An engineer wants to add per-connection state isolation to the HTTP transport. They invoke the Code Refinement panel before merging.

Four experts run SOLO, then converge through three CHALLENGE rounds at depth 3, gate through FEASIBILITY, and PERSIST to docs/panel-reviews/.

Sample finding from the persisted markdown

## Priya Sharma — Coupling

### Lens: where does state leak between sessions?

**Critical Findings**
1. SessionIsolation.loadSession() reads from a shared in-memory
   Map without per-session locking. Two concurrent connections can
   race on the same sessionId and observe a half-populated object.
   See src/SessionIsolation.ts:142.
   Consequence: under burst, the first request can see a state shape
   that the second request mutates between read and use.
   Alternative considered: serialize all reads through an async lock.
   Trade-off: adds tail latency under contention; acceptable here.

**Challenge to Natasha Volkov**
- You flagged this as a confidentiality breach. I read it as
  consistency. If the two sessions are same-tenant the data isn't
  leaking, it's racing. Can you sharpen the threat model?

Worked example 2 — Presentation panel against a research plan

A non-engineering use case. A team has produced a 12-page integration plan and needs a 15-minute stakeholder briefing. They invoke the Presentation panel in "extraction" mode.

Composition

Convened experts

  • Editor — narrative arc and signal-to-noise
  • Audience strategist — what the engineering leads will react to
  • Decision-deck specialist — what decisions need calling out vs. parking
  • Devil's advocate — what the room will push back on first
Inputs

What the panel reads

  • The 12-page plan as the artifact under review
  • The previous panel's persisted markdown if it exists
  • Stated audience: engineering team leads
  • Stated time budget: 15 minutes
Outputs

Final deliverable

  • Three-slide arc: situation, decision, ask
  • Five anticipated questions with one-line answers
  • Two flagged "do not promise" items
  • Persisted to docs/panel-reviews/YYYY-MM-DD/presentation-*.md
Why this works

Reasoning

The decision-deck specialist and devil's advocate together force the team to surface unresolved trade-offs before the meeting, instead of getting blindsided in it. The persistence step means the next person presenting a similar plan inherits the question list.

Where panels plug in

Panels are not standalone artifacts — they are injection points inside other workflows. A few of the mandatory ones:

Calling workflowPanelWhy mandatory
release-readinessSecurity → Testing → Performance cascadeNo production deploy without multi-panel GO/NO-GO.
repo-ingestorRepo IngestionAgent swarms produce unchecked claims; the panel validates accuracy.
docs-architectDocumentationPrevents aspirational docs shipping as fact.
orch-plan (3+ phases)ArchitectureComplex plans need sequencing and feasibility before commit.
tool-governanceSecurityNew tool approvals must be red-teamed before allowlisting.
orch-refactor (extract/rename)Code RefinementValidates that coupling and naming did not regress.

Anti-patterns to avoid

  • Don't run a panel for trivial decisions. A single expert is fine for a small change.
  • Don't skip the Challenge phase on critical reviews — that's where the best insights emerge.
  • Don't let panels become a rubber stamp. If every review ends with "looks good," the personas are not critical enough.
  • Don't ignore dissent — minority opinions from qualified experts are often prophetic.
  • Don't skip Feasibility. Brilliant recommendations that cannot be implemented are noise.
  • Don't present raw panel output to stakeholders. Always run it through the Presentation panel first.
