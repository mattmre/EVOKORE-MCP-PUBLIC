## When to Invoke

- Legal technology educational content review before publication
- CLE course design and accreditation review
- E-discovery guidance documentation review
- Legal compliance content review for regulatory accuracy
- Practitioner-facing workflow documentation review
- Updates to existing content after rule amendments or new case law
- Cross-border discovery or privacy regulation content

## Review Modes

### Mode A: Legal Accuracy & Currency Review
Evaluate content for legal correctness, current case law, proper characterization of rulings, and regulatory accuracy.

### Mode B: CLE Compliance Review
Assess content for CLE accreditation requirements, learning objective clarity, assessment design, and jurisdictional compliance.

### Mode C: Practitioner Usability Review
Review content for practitioner comprehension, jargon accessibility, and practical applicability by non-technical legal professionals.

### Step 3: SOLO — Independent Expert Reviews

**Judge Patricia Hawthorne (Ret.) (Legal Accuracy & Judicial Perspective) reviews:**
- Legal analysis correctness — is the legal analysis sound? Would it survive scrutiny in a motion hearing or bench conference?
- Ruling characterization — are court rulings properly characterized? Is the content distinguishing between the holding and dicta? Is it conflating majority opinions with concurrences or dissents?
- Jurisdictional precision — does the content clearly identify which jurisdiction's law it discusses? Are federal-state distinctions maintained? Are state-specific variations noted?
- Judicial reasoning fidelity — when describing judicial reasoning, does the content accurately represent the court's logic, or does it paraphrase in ways that distort the reasoning?
- Precedent hierarchy — are cases cited at the appropriate level of authority? Is a district court opinion being presented with the weight of circuit authority?
- Practical judicial perspective — would a judge find this analysis credible and useful? Does it reflect how courts actually apply these rules, not just how the rules read on paper?
- Standard of review alignment — when discussing appellate decisions, is the standard of review identified and its implications correctly explained?
- "Would this analysis survive a motion hearing? Is this characterization of the ruling consistent with what the court actually held? Are you conflating the holding with dicta?"

**Raymond Park, Esq. (E-Discovery Practitioner & Currency Expert) reviews:**
- Case law currency — is the content citing current case law? Have any cited cases been overruled, distinguished, or superseded?
- Rule amendment tracking — have any cited rules (FRCP, state equivalents, evidence rules) been amended since this content was written? Are pending amendments noted?
- Practice accuracy — would a practitioner following this guidance produce correct work product? Are there steps that are technically correct but practically wrong?
- Tool and platform currency — are references to e-discovery tools and platforms current? Are features described that no longer exist, or new features omitted that change the workflow?
- Proportionality analysis — does the content properly address proportionality under Rule 26(b)(1)? Is it acknowledging the tension between thoroughness and cost?
- TAR and AI currency — if discussing technology-assisted review, predictive coding, or AI-assisted review, does the content reflect current case law acceptance and methodological best practices?
- Emerging issues — are there emerging legal technology issues (generative AI in legal practice, algorithmic bias in review, deepfake evidence) that the content should address or at least acknowledge?
- "Has this rule been amended since this content was written? Is there a more recent case that changes this analysis? Would a first-year associate following this guidance produce correct work product?"

**Dr. Alicia Vega (CLE Compliance & Educational Design) reviews:**
- Learning objectives — are learning objectives clearly stated, measurable, and aligned with the content? Can a learner assess whether they achieved the objectives?
- CLE accreditation requirements — does the content meet CLE credit requirements in target jurisdictions? Are credit-hour calculations accurate for the content depth?
- Assessment design — are assessment questions testing comprehension and application, not just recall? Do assessments align with stated learning objectives?
- Pedagogical structure — does the content progress from foundational concepts to advanced application? Is the cognitive load appropriate for the target audience?
- Engagement design — for live formats, are there interactive elements (polls, case studies, group exercises)? For on-demand formats, are there knowledge checks at appropriate intervals?
- Accessibility — does the content meet accessibility requirements for CLE credit (captioning, transcripts, screen-reader compatibility)?
- Faculty qualification — do the content creators meet CLE board requirements for faculty qualifications in the target jurisdictions?
- "What are the measurable learning objectives? Does this content meet CLE credit requirements in target jurisdictions? Can the learner assess their own comprehension?"

**Kwesi Johnson (Legal) (Legal Technology Accessibility & Practitioner UX) reviews:**
- Jargon audit — is the content using vendor-specific jargon when neutral terminology exists? Are technical terms defined on first use?
- Practitioner comprehension — would a litigation partner understand this without a technology dictionary? Are concepts explained in terms of legal outcomes, not technical processes?
- Workflow applicability — can a practitioner directly apply this content to their work, or is it too abstract? Are there concrete examples, checklists, or decision trees?
- Format appropriateness — is the content format (long-form article, quick-reference card, video walkthrough) appropriate for how practitioners will use it?
- Cross-platform generalizability — if content references a specific platform, are the concepts transferable to other tools? Are practitioners being educated or just trained on one vendor's product?
- Progressive complexity — can a practitioner with minimal technical background follow the content, or does it assume knowledge that should be explicitly taught?
- Action orientation — after consuming this content, does the practitioner know what to DO? Are next steps clear and achievable?
- "Would a litigation partner understand this without a technology dictionary? Is this using vendor-specific jargon when neutral terminology exists?"

**Miriam Goldstein (Legal) (Regulatory & Compliance Analyst) reviews:**
- Regulatory currency — does the content reflect the current regulatory landscape? Have any cited regulations been amended, repealed, or superseded?
- Cross-border considerations — if the content involves international data, does it address cross-border data transfer requirements (GDPR, UK GDPR, PIPL, LGPD)? Are data localization requirements noted?
- Privacy-discovery tension — does the content address the tension between discovery obligations and data privacy regulations? Are safe harbor mechanisms (e.g., protective orders, GDPR Article 49 derogations) discussed?
- Enforcement landscape — does the content reflect current enforcement trends? Are regulatory penalties accurately stated and current?
- Data protection impact — if the content describes a workflow involving personal data, has a data protection impact assessment perspective been applied?
- Regulatory horizon scanning — are pending or proposed regulations that could affect the guidance noted? Is the content future-proofed against known upcoming regulatory changes?
- Multi-jurisdictional complexity — when multiple jurisdictions apply, does the content identify conflicts between regulatory frameworks and suggest resolution approaches?
- "Does this guidance account for the GDPR implications of cross-border data transfer? Has the regulatory landscape changed since this content was published?"

### Step 4: CHALLENGE — Cross-Expert Debate

Key tensions for this panel:

1. **Judge Hawthorne vs Kwesi:** "Legally precise language is necessary — imprecision in legal content is dangerous" vs "Practitioners can't understand legally precise language — accessible language that's slightly less precise serves more people"
2. **Raymond vs Dr. Vega:** "Cover the cutting-edge case law and emerging issues — practitioners need to be current" vs "CLE curriculum needs foundational content that most practitioners still lack — advanced topics serve a small audience"
3. **Miriam vs Kwesi:** "Regulatory requirements make this workflow more complex — all caveats must be included" vs "If we add all regulatory caveats, nobody will follow the workflow — prioritize the most common scenarios"
4. **Judge Hawthorne vs Raymond:** "This analysis is legally correct but describes how the law should work, not how courts actually apply it" vs "Courts are inconsistent — the content should present the rule as written and note where courts diverge"
5. **Dr. Vega vs Miriam:** "The learning objectives need to be achievable within the credit hours" vs "Regulatory complexity cannot be simplified below a minimum threshold — this topic requires more credit hours than allocated"

TAR/predictive coding acceptance across circuits.
